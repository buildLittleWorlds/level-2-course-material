Big Question

When is it helpful to have a machine
read your feelings — and when is it creepy?

Two Scenarios

The Journal Coach

You journal every day. An AI reads each entry and shows you patterns: "your mood has been declining over the last two weeks."

You chose to share. The tool reflects back to you.
The Classroom Camera

A school installs cameras that scan students' faces. An AI flags students who look "angry" or "distressed." The principal gets an alert.

You didn't choose. The system surveils.

What's the Difference?

Consent — the journal writer chose to share. The student didn't.
Agency — the journal tool reflects back. The camera watches.
Accuracy — journal words are chosen. A face in class is a frozen instant.
Power — who controls the data? Who sees the results?

Same technology. The design and context change everything.

The Redesign Steps

1. Title: "Sentiment Analysis" → "Restaurant Review Analyzer"
2. Description: technical jargon → plain English for a restaurant owner
3. Examples: random sentences → real restaurant reviews
4. Output: "POSITIVE / NEGATIVE" → "Happy Customer / Unhappy Customer" + advice
5. Visual: bare decimal → confidence bar

The Output Transformation

    if label == "POSITIVE":
        mood = "Happy Customer"
        if score > 0.95:
            advice = ("This customer loved the experience! "
                      "Consider thanking them publicly.")
        else:
            advice = ("This customer had a good experience. "
                      "A simple thank-you goes a long way.")

    confidence_bar = "█" * int(score * 20) + "░" * (20 - int(score * 20))
    return (
        f"Overall Impression:  {mood}\n"
        f"Confidence: {confidence_bar} {score:.0%}\n"
        f"\nSuggested Action:\n{advice}"
    )

Vocabulary

Prompt engineering — designing the entire experience around an AI model, not just the chat prompt
UX (user experience) — how a person experiences and interacts with a tool
User-centered design — designing for a specific audience's needs, not for programmers
Audience — the specific person you're designing for
Usability testing — watching real people try your tool to find what's confusing
