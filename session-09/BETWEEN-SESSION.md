## Part 1: Hub Challenge — Redesign for a Real Person

In class we did **user-centered design** — taking the same model and redesigning the interface for a specific audience. We saw how the same sentiment model goes from "POSITIVE 0.97" to "Happy Customer — consider thanking them publicly" just by changing the framing.

Now apply the same thinking to **your own interest**.

### Option A: Use a Template

1. Pick one of the four templates we saw in class (or invent your own):
   - Restaurant Review Analyzer (sentiment → advice for restaurant owners)
   - Email Tone Checker (tone detection → revision tips for professionals)
   - Journal Mood Tracker (emotion detection → reflection prompts for journalers)
   - Social Media Vibe Check (tone detection → posting advice for social media users)
2. Fork one of your existing Spaces (or start fresh) and redesign it for your chosen audience
3. For your redesign, answer the three questions:
   - **Who is this for?** (a specific person, not "everyone")
   - **What problem does it solve?** (one sentence)
   - **What should the output say?** (not labels — advice)
4. Change at least: the title, the description, and the output labels
5. **Bonus:** Have someone outside the class try your Space. Don't explain it — just share the URL. What confused them?
6. **Update your Collection** with any interesting Spaces you discover

### Option B: Redesign from Your Collection

1. Pick a model or Space from your Collection that you've been exploring — any task type (generation, classification, summarization, image models, anything)
2. Imagine a **specific person** who would use this — not "everyone," but someone real (a teacher, a musician, a younger sibling, a store owner)
3. Answer the three questions:
   - **Who is this for?**
   - **What problem does it solve?**
   - **What should the output say?**
4. You don't have to build it yet — just **design it on paper**:
   - What would the title be?
   - What would the description say?
   - What example inputs would you provide?
   - How would you reframe the output for your audience?
5. If you want to build it: fork the model's demo Space or start a new one and change the title, description, examples, and output labels
6. **Update your Collection** — add a tasting note about who could use this model and how you'd redesign it

The key idea is the same either way: **the model doesn't change — the design changes everything.** The same AI tool can be confusing or useful depending on who you're designing for and how you present the output.

### What to Notice

- Does the redesigned version feel more useful than the original?
- What's the hardest part of designing for a specific person?
- What information does your audience need that a programmer wouldn't?

## Part 2: Research Journal Entry

Add your Week 9 entry to `research-journal.md` in your GitHub repo. Same format as last week — 300-500 words.

### Week 9 Entry

```markdown
## Week 9 — User-Centered Design and Usability Testing

### This Week's Method
(What research method did we learn? Hint: user-centered design — redesigning AI output for a specific audience and testing whether it's useful.)

### How I Applied It
(What model or Space did you redesign? Who did you design it for? What did you change about the title, description, examples, or output?)

### What I Expected
(Before redesigning — did you think the changes would make a big difference? Did you expect it to be easy or hard?)

### What I Found
(What happened? Did the redesigned version feel more useful? If you tested it with someone, what confused them?)

### Why I Think This Happened
(Your explanation. Why does framing matter so much? What does this tell you about how people interact with AI tools?)

### Limitations
(What couldn't you test? How would you evaluate whether your redesign actually helped the target audience?)

### What I Want to Try Next
(You're building from scratch next week. What model and audience are you leaning toward? What will you design?)
```

If you're not sure what to write, start with: who did you design for, what did you change, and did it feel more useful? That's the core of user-centered design.
