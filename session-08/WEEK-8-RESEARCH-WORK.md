# Week 8 Research Work

Prereq: your `week-07-source-search.md` shortlist.

## This week's work: reading one paper with AI as a partner

Actually reading one paper. Not summarizing it. Not asking an AI "what does this paper say and should I cite it." Reading it yourself, with an AI as a *reading partner with guardrails* — a partner that explains terms and walks you through the methods section, but does NOT tell you what the authors concluded.

If you let an AI summarize a paper before you've read it, the AI has already decided what matters. You become a reader of the AI, not a reader of the paper. Your brief will end up vague and your citations will drift. We saw error propagation in an earlier session; the same thing happens when you read a paper through a hallucinating summarizer.

## What to do before Session 9

Pick **one paper** from your Week 7 shortlist — the one most directly on your question. Create `week-08-paper-read.md` with the title, authors, year, and URL/DOI at the top. Then do four structured steps:

### 1. Field orientation (AI-assisted, on the abstract only)

> I'm reading this paper for a short research brief. Do NOT tell me what the paper concluded or whether its findings are important. For this abstract: (1) name the field and one or two sibling fields it draws on, (2) define any 3–5 technical terms a bright high school student wouldn't know, in one sentence each, (3) tell me what kind of evidence the authors are promising (experimental? observational? a new dataset? a new method?) — without telling me what they found.
> """[paste abstract]"""

### 2. Methods walkthrough (AI-assisted, on the methods section only)

> Walk me through what the authors actually did — procedurally, step by step. Do NOT tell me what they found. If there's a statistical method I probably don't know, give me a one-sentence definition.
> """[paste methods paragraphs]"""

### 3. Read the results section YOURSELF

Don't paste it into an AI. In your own words, 3–5 sentences: what did they actually find? Note anything you didn't understand.

### 4. Read the limitations YOURSELF

2–4 sentences on the main limitations the authors name. If there's no limitations section, note *that* and be suspicious.

Finally, one paragraph: *What claim from this paper would you actually cite in your brief, and what would it support?* That's the hinge for Week 9.

## Watch out for

- Don't paste the whole paper into AI. The AI only sees the abstract and the methods. You read the results and limitations.
- If the AI spoils the findings anyway, re-prompt: "Back up — you told me the findings. Only explain the methods."
- Equations are a legitimate AI translation target: "What is this computing intuitively, in one sentence?"
- If the paper feels too hard, switching papers mid-week is fine. Better to read one paper well than grind through the wrong one.
