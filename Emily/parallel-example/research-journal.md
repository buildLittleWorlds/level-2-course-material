## Week 1 — Discovery of live conflict-tracking AI

In the second week of the course I came across a live conflict-tracking AI program. It generated near-real-time summaries of geopolitical incidents, drawing on multiple news sources, and could update its account of an event as new reporting came in.

What I noticed was that the tool *felt* authoritative — the interface was clean, the maps were impressive, the summaries read smoothly — but the more I checked it against the underlying source articles, the more I noticed that the AI's summary often picked one framing of the event and left the others out. A border incident summarized as a "security operation" in one source and as a "raid" in another would land in the AI summary as one or the other, not both, and there was no signal to the reader about which framing the AI had chosen or why.

This raised a question I didn't have language for yet: *if a model claims to summarize neutrally, what perspective is hiding inside the summary?* The Model UN side of my brain treated this as obvious — every summary takes a position, even when it claims not to — but the AI tool was presenting itself as if it had escaped that constraint.

I marked the question and moved on to other course material.

## Week 6 — Project pivot

After several weeks of working toward a global conflict tracker (an extension of the live-tracking tool from Week 1), I realized the project wasn't going to do what I wanted it to do. The tracker idea was technically interesting but not actually useful: it could surface what was happening, but it didn't help a general-public reader understand *why* the events were being reported the way they were. It made the news easier to access but not easier to think about.

The pivot came from my daily news subscriptions. I'd been reading **Ground News**, which doesn't try to deliver neutral news — instead it shows the political leaning of different sources covering the same story, surfaces public opinion, and lets readers see at a glance how the same event is being framed across the spectrum. That's the move I wanted: not pretending neutrality, but making bias visible on purpose.

The pivoted project became a personalized news newsletter with four pillars:

1. **Neutral summary**, generated by AI, of the day's most important stories.
2. **Per-article bias detection** — AI-detected political leaning on each individual article, not just on the outlet, so a center-leaning outlet's coverage of a contentious story gets its own per-article score.
3. **Sentiment collection** — a prompt asking the reader what they noticed *after* reading the bias-aware version, so the tool builds a small dataset of how bias-aware presentation affects reader response.
4. **User journal** — a way to save and annotate stories worth returning to.

This is a tool I would actually use. That's the test that the conflict-tracker idea failed and this idea passes.

The Week 6 entry also noted three reference points: the Euractiv article *"Rise of AI summaries risks weakening news brands, warns Reuters Institute"* (which framed the design problem at the news-industry level), Google AI Studio (as a build environment), and a working note that bias in AI tools comes from statistical patterns in training data, which is the underlying mechanism the literature on algorithm aversion assumes.

## Week 7 — Session 8 in-class test (worked example)

*This is a model entry showing what the Session 8 work will produce. Replace it with what we actually do in class.*

In Session 8 I picked one news event I'd been following — a UN vote with clear competing interpretations — and ran the same article through Claude three times, asking each time for a summary written from a different stated perspective: a humanitarian NGO, a national-security adviser, and a youth climate delegate. I saved the three outputs.

Walking through them with Dr. Plate using the framing table from the paper draft (shared facts, word choice, centered actor, main risk, implied solution), the strongest pattern was that the *facts* were largely the same across all three summaries. The vote happened, the same parties were named, the same numbers appeared. What changed was:

- **Word choice**: the NGO version used "civilian" and "displacement"; the security version used "regional stability" and "infrastructure"; the youth delegate version used "intergenerational" and "responsibility."
- **Centered actor**: the NGO summary centered the population affected; the security summary centered states and ministries; the youth delegate summary centered the future.
- **Implied solution**: the three versions implied three different next moves, even though none of them stated a recommendation outright.

The takeaway for the paper: *perspective prompts don't rewrite the facts, they reshape which facts feel most important.* That's the same finding the paper draft already names, but now it's grounded in a real test instead of a hypothetical one.

This entry will also serve as the comparison baseline for the Mode A vs. Mode B test in Session 9 — once the bias-detection pillar is built, I can run the same UN-vote article through the bias-aware tool and see whether the cross-spectrum quotes the model surfaces match the perspective shifts I observed when I prompted Claude directly.
