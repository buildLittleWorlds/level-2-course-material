# Project Evaluation Rubric

## From Paper to Project — Phase 4 Final Projects

This rubric is used to evaluate the student research Spaces built in Phase 4. It assesses both the technical execution and the intellectual engagement with the QED-Nano research.

---

## Scoring Overview

Each criterion is scored on a 4-point scale:

| Score | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| 4 | Excellent — exceeds expectations, shows depth and polish |
| 3 | Good — meets expectations, solid work |
| 2 | Developing — partially meets expectations, significant gaps |
| 1 | Beginning — attempt made, but major elements missing |

---

## Criterion 1: The Space Works (Technical Execution)

*Does the Space function as intended?*

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 4 | Space loads reliably, handles multiple inputs without errors, and responds in a reasonable time. Edge cases (empty input, unusual text) are handled gracefully. |
| 3 | Space works correctly for typical inputs. Minor issues may exist (slow loading, occasional error on unusual input) but core functionality is solid. |
| 2 | Space works for some inputs but fails for others. Core functionality is present but unreliable. |
| 1 | Space does not load, crashes frequently, or does not produce meaningful output. |

---

## Criterion 2: Connection to the Research (Intellectual Engagement)

*Does the project demonstrate understanding of the QED-Nano paper?*

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 4 | The project clearly investigates a specific aspect of the research (training method, evaluation approach, model comparison, data quality, or reasoning structure). The student can explain what they learned about the research from building this. |
| 3 | The project connects to the research in a clear way. The student understands the relevant part of the paper and can explain the connection. |
| 2 | The project has a loose connection to the research but doesn't engage deeply with any specific aspect. The student's understanding of the paper is surface-level. |
| 1 | The project doesn't meaningfully connect to the QED-Nano research. It could have been built without reading the paper. |

---

## Criterion 3: Designed for an Audience (Communication)

*Can someone who hasn't read the paper use this Space and learn something?*

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 4 | Interface text clearly explains what the Space does and why it matters. Labels, descriptions, and any instructions are written for a non-expert audience. A visitor learns something about AI reasoning just from using the Space. |
| 3 | Interface includes adequate explanation. A visitor can understand what the Space does, though some context may be missing. |
| 2 | Minimal interface text. The Space functions but a visitor wouldn't understand what they're looking at without external explanation. |
| 1 | No meaningful interface text. Raw inputs and outputs with no context. |

---

## Criterion 4: Thoughtful Choices (Design and Methodology)

*Did the student make deliberate decisions about how to build this?*

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 4 | The student made clear, justified choices: which problems to test, what criteria to evaluate, how to display information, what to include or exclude. They can explain *why* they made these choices. |
| 3 | The student made reasonable choices and can explain most of them. The Space reflects some thoughtful design decisions. |
| 2 | Choices appear arbitrary or default. The student followed a template without adapting it to their specific project. |
| 1 | No evidence of deliberate design decisions. |

---

## Criterion 5: Reflection (What Did You Learn?)

*Can the student articulate what they learned from this project?*

This criterion is assessed through a short written reflection or conversation, not through the Space itself. The student should be able to answer:

- What did you learn about the QED-Nano research from building this?
- What was the hardest part of the project, and how did you solve it?
- If you had more time, what would you improve or add?
- What did this project teach you about AI that you didn't know before?

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 4 | Thoughtful, specific answers that show genuine learning. The student identifies concrete things they understand now that they didn't before. |
| 3 | Adequate reflection with some specific insights. The student can describe what they learned. |
| 2 | Generic or vague answers. "I learned about AI" without specifics. |
| 1 | No meaningful reflection. |

## Notes for the Instructor

**The intellectual engagement criterion (Criterion 2) carries the most weight** because this module is fundamentally about learning through building. A technically polished Space that doesn't connect to the research misses the point; a rough but intellectually engaged Space that shows real understanding of the paper is a better outcome.

**The reflection (Criterion 5) can be a conversation** rather than a written assignment. For some students, especially younger ones, a 5-minute conversation about their project will reveal more about their understanding than a written paragraph.

**Be flexible about technical sophistication.** A student who builds the Training Data Explorer (Project C, the most accessible option) and does it thoughtfully — with clear interface text, good data display, and genuine engagement with what training data means — should score as well as a student who builds the Reasoning Trace Analyzer (Project E, the most ambitious option) but doesn't explain it well. The rubric intentionally values understanding and communication alongside technical execution.
