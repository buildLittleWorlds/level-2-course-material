A Word About Domain

Every AI model lives in a domain — the world of its training data.

Your research topic is also a domain.
Music. Medicine. Politics. Visual art. Animation.

When you pick a topic, you're choosing a domain.
When your model struggles with your topic, that's domain shift —
and that's where the interesting research questions live.

What Makes a Good Research Question?

It has an answer you don't already know.
"Can AI generate text?" → you already know. Try: "under what conditions" or "for whom."
It has wrong answers you can picture.
If there's no way to be wrong, there's no way to learn anything.
It's narrow enough to actually chase.
You have 5 weeks. One question. Three Spaces to test it.

Sharpening: Broad → Narrow

Broad: "Does AI work for music?"
Too big. No testable answer.
Medium: "Do text models distinguish opera vocabulary from jazz vocabulary?"
Getting there. What would you measure?
Narrow: "When given opera vs. jazz lyrics, does distilgpt2 produce stylistically distinct continuations, or does it flatten both into generic music text?"
Testable. Specific. Could be wrong.

Your RESEARCH-PATH.md has three candidates like this for your topic.

Your Question Lives in a Domain

Every one of your questions lives in a specific world —
music, medicine, politics, images, animation.

AI models also live in specific worlds:
the data they were trained on.

The most interesting research questions are about what happens
at the boundary — where your world
and the model's world don't quite match.

Vocabulary

Research Question — a specific, testable question with an answer you don't already know
Domain — the world a model was trained on; also the subject area your research lives in
Domain Shift — when real-world data differs from training data; where interesting findings live
External Validity — whether a finding holds up outside the specific conditions it was tested in
