# Week 6 Research Work

Through Session 5 we were experimenting — building Spaces, trying prompts, playing with what AI can do when you ask it the right way. That experimental phase was real and it had a purpose: you now have enough hands-on intuition to tell when AI is helping and when it's just producing noise. Starting this week we pick up the pace and turn that intuition toward producing researched quality material. By Session 10 you'll have drafted a short research brief grounded in real peer-reviewed sources — the kind of thing you could show someone as proof that you can do research with AI as an accelerator rather than a substitute.

## This week's work: sharpening a research question

A good research question has an answer you don't yet know, has wrong answers you can picture, and is narrow enough to actually chase. AI is excellent as a question-sharpening partner. It is terrible as an answer-generator at this stage, and you should not let it become one.

## What to do before Session 7

1. Pick one observation from tonight's class or from your Space work so far that feels *interesting* to you — not "correct," interesting. Write it as one sentence.
2. Run a question-sharpening conversation with Claude or ChatGPT. Ask for three candidate questions at different levels of specificity (broad / medium / narrow) and what evidence would count for each. Save the conversation into a file called `week-06-research-question.md` in your portfolio repo.
3. Pick the candidate that feels most promising and write one paragraph underneath it: what makes you want to chase it, and what would success look like?
4. Bring the file to Session 7.

## A good prompt to start from

> I'm a high school student in an applied AI course. I'm interested in [your observation]. I want to write a short research brief on this but I don't have a research question yet — I just have the observation. Can you help me sharpen this into a researchable question? Don't give me the answer. Give me three candidate questions at different levels of specificity, and explain what would count as evidence for each one.

## Watch out for

- "My AI just gave me the answer." → Different verb. "Help me *sharpen*," not "answer."
- "All three candidate questions were the same." → Re-prompt with an explicit spread: one broad, one medium, one narrow.
- "My question is 'does domain shift happen.'" → You already know the answer. Try "how much," "under what conditions," "for whom," or "why specifically."

## For an example

See Prea's [Week 1–4 journal entries](../example-student-prea/research-journal.md) for how a research question can sharpen across several weeks. Prea started with a vague hunch that "the model sees what I said but not how I said it" and ended Week 4 with a formal hypothesis about acoustic-prosodic features correlating with listener ratings of persuasiveness. You don't have to get that far in one week — but her early entries show what the sharpening process actually looks like.
