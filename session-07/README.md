## What This Session Covers

Students use AI to get from:

- a Space or project
- journal entries or rough notes
- a research question or half-formed question
- a personalized `paper-starter.md`

to a **rough `PAPER.md` draft** in one session.

The important change is that the paper flow is now **prompt-first**:

- students do not fill in blanks
- students do not hand-compose the first version section by section
- students use the shared `PAPER-TEMPLATE.md` as a master AI prompt
- students then critique, correct, and revise what the AI gives back

## The core teaching line

The line for this session is:

> The AI gets you from materials to draft. Your job is to check whether the draft sounds like your project.

That means the real work of the session is not "writing from zero." It is:

1. gather evidence
2. run the prompt
3. inspect the result
4. revise the most generic and least honest sections

## What students should leave with

Every student should leave with some version of `PAPER.md`.

For stronger students, that means a rough but real draft.

For earlier students, that means a rough draft with provisional sections that clearly say the project is still early.

The key is that everyone leaves with a draft they can revise, not a blank page and not an outline unless they are truly unable to produce even a provisional paper.

## Where fairness still lives

The official concept is still bias and fairness in AI.

It shows up in the paper flow mainly through the limitation question:

- who might this tool work less well for?
- what kind of input does it flatten?
- what part of the model or design is shaped by training data bias or domain mismatch?

So even though the notebook is not the main live activity, the fairness theme remains a real pressure on the draft.

## What success looks like

A successful Session 7 draft:

- states what the student built
- names one real research question
- includes one test or example
- says what the student learned
- includes one honest limitation

It does not need to be polished. It does need to sound like it came from the student’s actual work.
