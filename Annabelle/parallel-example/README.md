# Parallel Example: Music Genre Prompt Lab

This packet models how a music-focused Hugging Face Space idea could become a research journal and a short paper-lite draft. It sits beside the student's actual music work, but it does not replace it or claim to describe what the student built.

## Core idea

The imagined Space is a small **Music Genre Prompt Lab**. A user chooses a model, a genre label, and a short musical-writing task. The Space generates text that is supposed to sound like opera, jazz, or classical music writing. The research question asks whether the model is doing more than swapping in genre vocabulary.

## Research question

When small instruction-tuned models are asked to write in opera, jazz, and classical styles, do they produce genre-specific structure, or do they mostly recycle generic music words?

## Space idea

Inputs:

- Genre: opera, jazz, or classical
- Task: "write a scene prompt," "write a rehearsal note," or "write a short performance description"
- Model choice: a small base generator and one or two instruction-tuned models

Outputs:

- Generated text
- A simple checklist for genre markers
- A short reflection prompt asking what feels specific, generic, or musically misleading

## What this packet demonstrates

- How to turn "AI and music" into a testable question about genre specificity
- How to use source-search results as a research neighborhood, not as decoration
- How journal entries can move from curiosity to a concrete scoring method
- How a paper-lite draft can make a cautious claim without pretending the experiment is larger than it is

## How to use this example

Read the files in this order:

1. `sources.md` for the research neighborhood
2. `research-journal.md` for the week-by-week question sharpening
3. `PAPER.md` for the final draft shape

Borrow the structure, not the claims. A real student paper should use that student's own prompts, outputs, tests, and source verification.
