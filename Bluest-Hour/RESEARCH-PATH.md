## Step 1: Read the journal like evidence

Do not read the Bluest Hour journal as a polished essay. Read it as a record of decisions:

- Week 1: the problem was badly framed because sunset was too early
- Week 2: the build got clearer when the project became a centered 20-minute walk
- Week 3: the local offset made the app more honest to place
- Weeks 4–5: the journal feature created a model-fit problem
- Week 6: the model-fit problem became a research question
- Week 7: the limitations became visible enough to write the paper

That is the whole move students need to learn.

## Step 2: Pull out the question

The strongest question in the Bluest Hour journal is not:

> Is this a nice app?

It is:

> When people write short reflections after blue-hour-timed walks, does a generic positive/negative sentiment model actually capture their mood, or does it flatten a more mixed reflective style of writing?

That question works because it is:

- narrow
- tied to the existing build
- grounded in actual observations from the journal

## Step 3: Map the journal to the paper

The short paper is built directly from the journal:

| Paper section | Journal source |
|---|---|
| **What I built** | Weeks 2–3 |
| **My research question** | Week 6 |
| **Why this matters to me** | the motivation visible across Weeks 1–6 |
| **What I tried** | Weeks 4–5 |
| **What I learned** | Weeks 5–7 |
| **What still needs work / who it might fail for** | Week 7 |

This is the pattern students should copy with their own work.

## Step 4: What counts as a good paper move here

For this example, the good move is not literary flourish. The good move is clarity:

- name the project
- state the question
- describe one real test
- say what changed in your thinking
- name one honest limitation

If a student can do those five things, the paper is working.

## Step 5: Source search directions

The Bluest Hour question lives near a few real research neighborhoods:

- nature exposure and mood
- evening light / timing / wellbeing
- reflective writing and journaling
- sentiment analysis on diary-like or ambiguous text

That is enough to support a small student paper. The goal is not to master the entire literature. The goal is to show that the question belongs to a real research neighborhood.

## Step 6: The advanced continuation

The almanac redesign is still useful, but now it should be read as **Stage 4**, not Stage 1.

The sequence is:

1. original project
2. research journal
3. simple paper
4. advanced redesign / advanced paper

That keeps the student target realistic while still preserving the more ambitious Bluest Hour work as something worth growing toward.
